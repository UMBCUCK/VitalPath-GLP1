### TITLE: The PCOS–Insulin Resistance Loop That Actually Drives Weight Gain (And What To Do About It)

**Subhead:** A clinician's evidence-based look at why PCOS makes weight loss two or three times harder — and where GLP-1 medications fit into the picture.

**Author byline:** [Dr. Name, Specialty], Medical Director, Nature's Journey

---

About 1 in 10 women of reproductive age has polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), the most common endocrine disorder in this population. It is also the most under-diagnosed: research published in *The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism* estimates that **up to 70% of women with PCOS are undiagnosed**.

If you have PCOS — or you suspect you do — and you have been told to "just eat less and exercise more," this article is for you. Because that advice, applied to a PCOS body, is not just unhelpful. It is actively misleading.

Here is what is happening biochemically when a woman with PCOS tries to lose weight, why the standard playbook fails, and where newer treatments — including GLP-1 medications — fit into a realistic plan. Quick disclosure up front: I am the medical director of a telehealth practice that prescribes compounded GLP-1 medications, which are not FDA-approved. I will explain that distinction below and the article is written to be useful regardless of whether anyone reading it ever uses our service.

---

#### The loop, in plain language

PCOS is not a single disorder. It is a syndrome — a collection of features that often appear together. The diagnostic criteria (the Rotterdam criteria, used by most US endocrinologists) require two of three:

- Irregular or absent menstrual cycles
- Elevated androgens (testosterone, DHEA-S) on bloodwork or visible signs (acne, hirsutism, scalp thinning)
- Polycystic-appearing ovaries on ultrasound

What ties these features together is **insulin resistance** — and specifically, a kind of insulin resistance that is partially independent of body weight. A landmark study by Dunaif et al. (*Diabetes*, 1989) showed that lean women with PCOS have insulin resistance comparable to overweight women without PCOS. In other words: PCOS is, fundamentally, an insulin-signaling disorder. Weight is a downstream consequence, not the root cause.

Here's the loop:

1. **Insulin resistance** means cells respond poorly to insulin. The pancreas compensates by making more.
2. **High insulin** stimulates the ovaries to produce more testosterone, and reduces sex-hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), which means even more *free* testosterone is bioavailable.
3. **High testosterone** disrupts ovulation (the irregular cycles), drives the cosmetic features (acne, hirsutism), and — critically — promotes visceral fat storage.
4. **Visceral fat** is metabolically active. It releases inflammatory cytokines and free fatty acids that *worsen* insulin resistance.
5. Back to step 1, with the loop now tighter.

This is why women with PCOS so often describe weight loss as feeling impossible: their physiology is actively defending the weight they have, and every pound of visceral fat lost reduces the headwind for losing the next pound.

---

#### Why "calories in, calories out" understates the problem

The standard advice — create a calorie deficit, exercise, lose weight — is not wrong. It is just incomplete in a way that matters for PCOS.

A 2019 review in *Reproductive Biology and Endocrinology* synthesized data from 12 PCOS clinical trials and found that even with identical caloric intake and matched exercise prescriptions, women with PCOS lose, on average, **30–50% less weight** than weight-matched controls without PCOS. The mechanism is not metabolism — resting metabolic rate is broadly similar — it is the hormonal substrate.

Three specific factors:

- **Hyperinsulinemia** drives lipogenesis (fat storage) and inhibits lipolysis (fat release).
- **Elevated androgens** preferentially recruit fat storage to the abdominal/visceral compartment, which is the metabolically worst place to store it.
- **Disrupted appetite signaling** — emerging research suggests that women with PCOS have altered satiety signaling involving GLP-1 itself, leptin, and ghrelin.

The practical implication is that a calorie deficit produces less weight loss in a PCOS body, and the weight that does come off comes off in the wrong order (subcutaneous before visceral), which is not what you want for cardiometabolic risk reduction.

---

#### What actually works (in this order)

The interventions with the strongest evidence in PCOS are not the ones most often prescribed. Here is the rough hierarchy:

**1. Resolve the insulin resistance first.**

This is non-negotiable. Until insulin signaling improves, every other intervention is fighting upstream. Two evidence-based levers:

- **Metformin.** A 2017 Cochrane review (Tang et al.) confirmed metformin's role in PCOS for both weight and ovulation. It is cheap, FDA-approved (off-label for PCOS), and well-tolerated by most.
- **GLP-1 receptor agonists.** This is newer evidence. A 2022 meta-analysis in *Frontiers in Endocrinology* (Bednarz et al.) of 8 randomized trials in PCOS showed GLP-1 agonists outperformed metformin on weight loss (mean difference ≈3.5 kg) and improved insulin sensitivity. The largest of those trials used liraglutide; semaglutide-specific PCOS data is emerging but not yet published at the same scale.

Both metformin and GLP-1s have the same upstream target — they break the insulin loop. Some endocrinologists now combine them.

**2. Resistance training over cardio.**

Twice-weekly resistance training has been shown in PCOS-specific trials (Kogure et al., *Clinical Endocrinology*, 2016) to improve insulin sensitivity *independently of weight loss* — meaning the bloodwork improves before the scale moves. Cardio helps too, but if you have to pick one, lift.

**3. Carbohydrate quality, not necessarily quantity.**

The data on low-carb / keto in PCOS is suggestive but not conclusive. What is conclusive is that *glycemic load* matters: replacing refined carbs with whole-food carbohydrate sources (legumes, whole grains, vegetables, intact fruit) improves insulin response in PCOS. Most women with PCOS feel meaningfully better at a moderate-carb, high-protein, high-fiber pattern.

**4. Sleep and circadian regulation.**

Insulin sensitivity is tied to sleep. One week of partial sleep restriction reduces insulin sensitivity by about 30% in healthy adults (Spiegel et al., *Annals of Internal Medicine*, 2004). PCOS amplifies this. If you are sleeping less than 7 hours a night, no medication or diet plan will work optimally.

**5. Address androgens directly only after the loop is broken.**

Spironolactone, oral contraceptives, and topical anti-androgens have a role for the cosmetic features (acne, hirsutism, scalp thinning) but they do not break the insulin loop. They are downstream tools.

---

#### Where GLP-1 medications fit (and the honest caveats)

GLP-1 receptor agonists — semaglutide, tirzepatide, liraglutide — work in PCOS for the same reasons they work in non-PCOS obesity, plus one PCOS-specific reason. They:

- Slow gastric emptying (you feel full sooner)
- Reduce reward-driven eating ("food noise")
- Improve insulin sensitivity directly
- Lower fasting and post-meal glucose

For a woman with PCOS, the third bullet is the one that matters most. By improving insulin sensitivity, GLP-1s break the upstream loop driving everything else.

The honest caveats:

- **No GLP-1 is FDA-approved specifically for PCOS.** The FDA approvals are for type 2 diabetes (semaglutide as Ozempic, tirzepatide as Mounjaro) and chronic weight management (semaglutide as Wegovy, tirzepatide as Zepbound). PCOS use is off-label, even with branded medications.
- **Compounded versions are not FDA-approved at all.** When you see a telehealth practice (including ours) advertising affordable semaglutide or tirzepatide, those are typically compounded versions made by 503A or 503B pharmacies. They contain the same active ingredient as the branded drug, but the FDA has not reviewed the specific compounded preparation. This is a legitimate access pathway during shortages and for cash-pay patients, but it is not regulatorily equivalent to the branded medication.
- **Side effects are real.** GI side effects (nausea, constipation, occasionally vomiting) affect roughly a third of patients in the first month. They typically improve with dose titration. About 5–10% of patients discontinue due to side effects.
- **Stopping causes weight regain.** GLP-1s manage the underlying signaling — they do not cure PCOS or obesity. Most patients regain 50–75% of lost weight within a year of stopping (Wilding et al., *Diabetes, Obesity and Metabolism*, 2022). This is not a failure of the medication; it is a feature of how the underlying disease works.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding contraindicate GLP-1s.** Discontinue at least 2 months before attempting pregnancy. Do not use while breastfeeding.

---

#### What to ask your provider

If you have PCOS or suspect you do, the first call is to a primary-care provider or endocrinologist for proper evaluation. Bring this list:

- Fasting insulin (not just fasting glucose — fasting insulin is the test that detects insulin resistance early)
- HOMA-IR (calculated from fasting insulin and glucose)
- HbA1c
- Free testosterone, total testosterone, SHBG
- DHEA-S
- TSH (to rule out thyroid)
- 25-OH vitamin D
- Lipid panel

If insulin resistance is confirmed, ask about metformin first (it is generic, $4–$10/month, well-studied) and about a GLP-1 second if metformin alone is insufficient or not tolerated.

---

#### The bottom line

PCOS is not a willpower problem and it is not a "just lose weight" problem. It is a hormonal-signaling disorder with a biochemically self-reinforcing loop, and the interventions that work are the ones that target the loop — not the calorie count.

Insulin resistance is the upstream lever. Address it, and everything else (cycles, weight, cardiometabolic markers, even mood) tends to follow. Ignore it, and no amount of effort downstream will be enough.

If you have been working hard at this and feeling like nothing is moving — it is not you, it is the loop. There are tools that work. Start with the labs, find a clinician who actually understands PCOS, and pick the intervention that addresses the upstream cause.

## Part 5 — What NOT to Do

- **Never do link exchanges.** Google penalizes reciprocal linking, and it makes us look amateur.
- **Never pay for "guest posts on 50 sites for $99."** Those are PBNs. Google penalizes them, and recovery takes 6+ months.
- **Never pitch the same outlet twice in 30 days.** It poisons the relationship.
- **Never send a press release.** Editors trash 99% of them. Personal pitches only.
- **Never claim our compounded medication is "the same as" Ozempic, Wegovy, or Mounjaro.** Same active ingredient ≠ same medication. The footer disclosure is non-negotiable.
- **Never offer to write content "in exchange for a link."** Offer good content. If they want to link, they will. If not, the relationship is still preserved.
